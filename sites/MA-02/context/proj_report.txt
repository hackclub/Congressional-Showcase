Rationale
—————————————————————————————
Brain tumors are masses of abnormal cell growth in the brain caused by DNA mutations or other genetic factors. There are two types of tumors: non-cancerous and malignant. This study focuses on categorizing and locating malignant brain tumors like gliomas, pituitary tumors, and meningiomas. According to the American Brain Tumor Association, around 90,000 people get diagnosed with brain tumors every year. In the clinical world, quick and accurate diagnoses are essential, opposed to manual classification which can be prone to errors and very time-consuming.
According to the American Brain Tumor Society, brain tumors have the highest per-patient initial cost of care among all cancer types, with annualized net costs of around $150,000 (Nather 2). Excision of a small, benign tumor, alone, can cost around $50,000, while treatment for a malignant tumor, requiring combination of surgery, radiation and chemotherapy can exceed $700,000 (How Much Does Brain Tumor Treatment Cost? - CostHelper, 2024). These statistics prove that misdiagnosis in patients will lead to substantial financial burdens. A study found that more than 28% of oncology patients received treatment that deviated from evidence-based standards or lacked medical justification, contributing to a loss in billions of dollars towards unnecessary health expenditures annually (Naum 2013). These statistics demonstrate the impact that misdiagnosis of tumors have on the lives of many people, wasting time and money for unnecessary expenditures.
Deep learning models are known to mitigate the effect of misdiagnosis of brain tumors in patients; however, the large amount of redundant parameters they contain often leads to overfitting and inefficient learning, especially with limited medical datasets (MDPI Sensors, 2022). A study found that VGG-16, a pre-trained model with 138 million parameters, reported to have a lower accuracy on certain MRI-based brain tumor datasets compared to smaller, more optimized CNN architectures, demonstrating that size isn’t a determining factor in performance of the model. Another diminishing factor of other deep learning systems is that they don’t specialize in more than one task, which is crucial for implementation in the clinical environment.
In response to these problems, this study will create a multimodal, deep learning app that effectively and accurately classifies and localizes tumors based on MRI scans, optimizing diagnosis predictions and model size as well, while implementing each model in a user-friendly user interface for easier use in the medical field.

Literature Reviews

Ullah, M. (2024)
This study uses a multimodal deep learning structure to effectively classify and segment brain tumors based on MRI scans. It also uses a version of explainable AI, which is used for people to understand what’s going on in terms of brain tumors, a highly complex topic. This architecture contains 2 main frameworks. The first main framework architecture uses a pre-build DeepLabV3+ architecture with ASPP (Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling) to extract features. It also uses Bayesian optimization-based hyperparameter optimization to optimize hyperparameters and increase the performance of the model. The second proposed architecture contains custom classification models. IRB-96 and IRB-self models (Inverted Residual Bottleneck with 96 layers) and an IRB with self-attention. The dataset that was used to train the second framework is Figshare, a database with a wide variety of datasets. The accuracy of the first framework was around 92%, while the accuracy of the second framework was around 95%.

Karif, R. (2023)
This paper is a literature review that talks about the basics of brain tumors like white and grey matter along with CSP (Cerebrospinal Fluid). It examined the flaws and benefits of deep learning models as well as presented some statistics on how many studies there are with a model that does classification, segmentation or both. It also talks about types of models like GoogleNet, AlexNet, and VGG when deciding to create a classification model for classification tasks. The accuracies of these models were very high, ranging from 97+ percent accuracy for classification tasks. It also talks about using metrics like precision, F1 score, and sensitivity for further performance assessment.

Nazir, Md. (2024)
This paper talks about an AI model that is specifically used for brain tumor detection but has explainable AI built into it to increase transparency, preventing medical experts from “owing to their ‘black box’ nature” (Nazir et al. 2024). The model framework contains a fully custom-built CNN architecture and 3 explainable AI features: SHAP, LIME, and Grad-CAM. The study utilized a dataset containing 3000+ images that encompass both tumorous and non-tumorous cases. The model achieved an accuracy of 100% and a validation accuracy of 98.67%. A precision, recall, and F1 score metric also presented promising results of 98.50%. This indicated a promising future for the model and how it can greatly impact the world of neuroimaging.

Sivamurugan, V. (2024)
This paper talks about the effects of L2 regularization, or what’s known as ridge regression, on the performance of the EfficientNet classification model. This study utilized 2 datasets: Dermnet and the ISIC 2019 dataset. The researchers performed augmentation and oversampling to overcome imbalances and disproportionality in the datasets so that it wouldn’t hinder performance. The EfficientNet model was trained on each dataset. The model was trained on both datasets for two sequences: one with L2 regularization and one without. For the training sequence that did have L2 regularization, the model achieved an f1-score of 81.3% for the ISIC 2019 dataset and 61.4% for the Dermnet dataset.

Data-Centric AI & Model-Centric AI: Getting the best of both worlds
In many studies, most researchers assume that building heavier models with hefty computational costs is going to solve issues with model performance. This not only leads to overfitting, which leads to slower convergence, but also requires a lot of computational power to run, leading to slower prediction times. To combat this, this study took a different approach. While building NeuroLens, the data was approached first before building any model. Specifically, the data was fine tuned to maximum quality before being fed into an AI model. According to world renowned ML developer Andrew Ng, he advocates for “data-centric AI models”, which he describes as “the discipline of systematically engineering data needed to build a successful AI system” (Brown 22). This app uses a blend of this principle and traditional principles like model-centric AI when designing, building, and testing the classification, segmentation, and detection models, prioritizing both good data with good model architecture

Histogram Equalization
A digital image can be represented as a square matrix of numbers that correspond to a specific pixel intensity value on either the RGB scale or grayscale (black and white). When applying histogram equalization, a grayscale filter was applied to normalize RGB pixel intensities to values between 0 and 1. In this case, brighter pixels will have higher pixel intensity values (higher r values) while dimmer pixels have lower pixel intensity values (lower r values). However, there are certain boundaries that the pixel intensity values can lie between.
 
The variable m denotes the maximum number of bits required to represent the intensity level, and the variable L denotes the number of intensity values. The pixel intensity value r for each pixel in an N x N pixel matrix and its frequency can be plotted on a histogram.

Gaussian Blur
Even though histogram equalization might add a lot of contrast, it leaves off a lot of noise, which can cause the model to overfit as a result of noisy data. Gaussian blurring is a technique often used in machine learning to combat this issue. It is typically achieved by convolving an image using a Gaussian kernel (Misra and Wu, 2019). The Gaussian kernel is typically expressed as a 2 dimensional kernel that follows the following equation: 
                        
                                         
Figure C shows a visual representation of the 2D Gaussian curve the kernel follows
In simple terms, gaussian blurring takes the values of nearby pixels, averages them out and creates a uniform blur across those pixels, softening the image as a result. Gaussian blur filtering is considered a type of low-pass filter since it reduces high-frequency details and noise by smoothing out pixels in an image. The algorithm calculates the intensity of each pixel based on its nearby pixel intensity, giving more weight to nearby pixels and gradually decreasing the weight as the distance increases. This study uses a 5 x 5 kernel for efficient blurring and a sigma value of 0 to retain a constant distribution according to the 2D gaussian curve. With an image size of 128 x 128, it is important to retain a proper distribution of pixels when pre-processing these images.

Anisotropic Diffusion Filtering
Similar to Gaussian Blur, Anisotropic Diffusion Filtering (ADF) is a type of smoothing technique that selectively smooths areas with uniform regions while preserving sharp edges, critical in object detection, medical imaging, and medical segmentation. Gaussian blur is an isotropic blurring method, which means that it applies a constant blur across all of an image. However it fails to adapt to non-uniform edges and preserve them in an image. This is where ADF comes into play. ADF is a type of anisotropic diffusion, meaning that it adapts to local features of an image by smoothing more in flat edges and reducing smoothing near edges to prevent blurring of important structures, and especially tumors as well. 
	Mathematically, ADF adapts to the gradient of an image, which is the rate of change of pixel intensities across an image. A code snippet of the ADF implementation is shown below: 

In this implementation, the MRI scan had gaussian blur filtering and histogram equalization applied on it and is still converted into a numpy array of numbers. The following function “anisotropic_diffusion” takes in 4 arguments: the image array, the number of iterations, the lambda value, and the kappa value.
In the function, the given image array is converted into float to avoid overflow in calculations during convolutions. ADF detects intensity differences in the 4 cardinal directions (north, south, east, and west) of a given pixel. Therefore, 4 convolutional kernels are required for this computation and adaptation to blurs and noise. Then, delta_north, or the computed gradient between the pixel of reference and its neighboring pixels north of it. The same goes for delta_south, delta_east, and delta_west. This computed gradient is achieved by convolving the 4 convolutional kernels on the image in each direction of the referred pixel. After the computed gradients are attained, a conduction coefficient is calculated. A conduction coefficient is a coefficient that controls how much smoothing happens at or around the pixel of reference. This helps detect large gradients, which help detect edges better and distinguish them from smoothed regions which are diffused. A conduction coefficient is defined by a conduction function, or the Perona-Malik equation (Perona, P., & Malik, J. 1990). After conduction coefficients are calculated for all cardinal directions of the gradient of a pixel using each of the convolutional kernels, the image is updated with new conduction coefficients and gradients and as a result, blurs the image while preserving refined edges.

Laplacian Variance
A problem that was faced in this study was measuring the blurriness of a specific image, which is important in evaluating how well the model will process the image. If an image is too blurry, it will also hinder training the model; however, if an image has too much noise, it will have a negative impact on training as well. This is where OpenCV’s laplacian variance came into play. Laplacian variance is a constant that gives info on the spread of pixels. The laplacian variance is calculated after image pre-processing is applied. The laplacian variance, by definition, is a second-order derivative that is used to calculate the areas of rapid change of intensity values of pixels in an image. This is often approximated using a laplacian kernel, a 3x3 matrix that convolves with the input image. The result is the laplacian of the input image, which highlights areas of changes in pixel intensity values. To get a single, floating point number, the study took the variance of all laplacian values in the image, resulting in the laplacian variance. The lower the laplacian variance is, the blurrier the image is; however, the higher the laplacian variance is, the clearer the image appears to be. The laplacian variance is 250-300, which is ideal for medical imaging.

Class Weights

When training NeuroLens’ classification model, a major problem that was encountered was that it would bias its prediction towards the class that is most abundant in the dataset when making generalizations on unseen data. Particularly, the dataset the classification model was trained on had 3 classes: Glioma, Meningioma, and Pituitary Tumors. Gliomas dominated the dataset compared to other minority classes like Meningioma and Pituitary Tumors, which was why the model always defaulted to Glioma as its prediction. To solve this problem, class weights were implemented. This allows the model to focus more on minority classes like Meningiomas and Pituitary Tumors while training on the dataset. Furthermore, class weights prevent overfitting and bias towards dominating classes while maximizing results on the test dataset. Class weights were calculated using inverse frequency. The more abundant it is in a dataset, the less weight it gets. The same is true vice-versa.

Detection and Classification Model Selection
Detection is a binary task that outputs either a “yes” or a “no”. However, since it is working with images, the best type of model to work with is a neural network, particularly a convolutional neural network (CNN). A CNN is a type of feed-forward network that takes an input image and shrinks its dimensions until it is a 1-dimensional vector. This vector can be used for probability generation and decision thresholding to generate a binary value. For classification tasks, CNNs are also used; however, building it is very different. Usually, for multi-class classification, many classes have to be accounted for. This requires a similar structure, but a different output layer with a different amount of neurons and a different loss and activation function since it is multi-class and not binary.


Loss Functions
Loss functions are graphs that measure the performance of a specific model. Loss functions can be useful when finding certain fluctuations in the performance of a model, indicating either overfitting or underfitting if the loss is too large at times. Classification models use a type of loss function called a cross-entropy loss function, which measures the difference between the distribution of the predicted probability distribution and the actual probability distribution. Additionally, it penalizes the model for more incorrect, confident predictions. There are 2 types of cross-entropy loss functions: categorical and binary cross-entropy. Categorical cross-entropy loss functions are used for classification-based tasks, while binary cross-entropy loss functions are used for binary tasks like detection.

Batch Normalization
When a model overfits, it is usually due to it learning too much of the data that it learns the wrong patterns of the data and memorizes instead of familiarizing with the data and learning patterns off it. Batch Normalization (BN) normalizes the activations of a layer by calculating the mean and variance for each feature across a mini-batch. Then, the mean of the mini-batch is subtracted from the activation value of a layer and is by the standard deviation (with a small epsilon for numerical stability and to prevent division by 0). This reduces internal covariate shift, stabilizing training and improving convergence. Additionally, BN introduces trainable parameters to allow the model to learn optimal transformations after normalization. BN allows for stable training and faster learning rates, allowing for faster convergence as well.

L2 Regularization
L2 regularization, or ridge regression, is a type of regularization that prevents overfitting by penalizing large weights in the neural network. This penalty is applied to any large weight in the model, preventing the model from prioritizing any single feature, reducing its complexity and change of overfitting in data. Mathematically, the penalty term in L2 regularization is proportional to the sum of the squared values of the model’s weights.

Dropout
Dropout is a type of regularization technique that randomly deactivates a subset of neurons in a neural network, preventing overfitting by forcing a network to learn robust features of an image. It can be thought of as training multiple smaller networks simultaneously, since each training iteration uses a different subset of neurons. During training, the remaining neurons are scaled by a factor of 1/(1-dropout_rate), where dropout_rate is an adjustable parameter that dictates the percent of neurons randomly dropped from the network.


Optimizer
While the loss function is tracking the performance of the model, dips in the performance of a model can signal these optimizers to update the weights of a network in response to the loss to prevent loss in future occurrences. The most famous types of optimizers used are called stochastic gradient descent (SGD) optimizers. Based on the word “descent”, an SGD optimizer minimizes the loss given to the optimizer by the loss function, improving performance and convergence of the model. The primary optimizer used to build NeuroLen’s models is the Adam gradient-based optimizer, which is a type of stochastic gradient descent optimizer that updates the learning rate of each parameter based on the history of it’s gradients, leading to faster and more stable convergence as well.

Segmentation Model
Unlike the classification and detection models, segmentation models are models that generate a binary mask that separates a desired region against its background to help with localization. In this case, the segmentation model is going to be useful for localizing and drawing precise tumor boundaries for tumor excision during neurosurgery. 
NeuroLens’s segmentation model uses a custom-built, u-shaped architecture, or more famously known as the UNet. When building the segmentation model, there were two types of data that had to be dealt with: MRI scans and their corresponding binary masks. Firstly, images that didn’t have a tumor were filtered out through filtering out all masks that were black-only. These images were organized into a pandas dataframe using the pandas library in python, and were split into training, testing and validation directories for training purposes. 
After the data was organized, pre-processed, and ready, the model architecture of NeuroLens’ segmentation model was built. The segmentation model incorporates an encoder, bottleneck and decoder pathway that compresses, analyzes and decompresses an image into a single, binary mask that the model outputs. Once a basic architecture was ready, the model was trained and extensively iterated upon through the addition of regularization techniques like Batch Normalization, L2 regularization and Dropout layers to prevent over-segmentation of the model. After extensive iteration, it was then implemented into NeuroLens’ custom-built, user-friendly UI and tested on images extracted from the web to test its performance on data it hasn’t seen before.

Results
—————————————————————————————
After extensive training and iteration, NeuroLens performed with accuracies similar to that of industry leading models with only a fraction of the parameters, taking up less space than other models. NeuroLen’s classification model achieved a train accuracy of 99%, a test accuracy of 93% and a validation accuracy of 95%. Furthermore, it achieved a precision score of 0.97, a recall score of 0.96 and an f1-score of 0.96 for the glioma category. Additionally, it achieved a precision, recall and f1-score of 0.92, 0.94, and 0.93, respectively, for the meningioma category. In the pituitary tumor category, it achieved a precision, recall and f1-score of 0.99. Accounting for accuracy, precision, recall and f1-scores,  NeuroLen’s classification model averaged an accuracy of 96%. When comparing NeuroLens’ classification model to other models like VGG and DenseNet, it only differs by 1-2% in terms of accuracy; however, models like VGG and DenseNet are shown to have 3-60 times the amount of parameters that NeuroLens has, taking up substantial amounts of space and increasing the reliability of expensive cloud-based options for storage. 
For the NeuroLens’ detection model, it achieved a train accuracy of 99% and a test accuracy of 98%. It achieved a precision, recall, and f1-score of 0.96, 0.99, and 0.97 respectively for the “no-tumor” category. For the “tumor” category, it achieved a precision, recall and f1-score of 0.99, 0.96, and 0.97. Accounting the train and test accuracies, precision, recall, and f1-scores, NeuroLens’ detection model averaged an accuracy of 98%. When comparing NeuroLens’ detection model to other models like FCN-AlexNet and VGG-16, it outperforms them even though models like them take up 25-60 times the amount of space NeuroLen’s detection model takes up. NeuroLens’ segmentation model achieved an overall IoU (intersection over union) score of 0.85, passing industry leading models as well while only taking up a fraction of the space other models like FCN-AlexNET and DeepLab V3+ take up.

I. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
—————————————————————————————

Current Limitations:
1. Model trained primarily on axial plane images; sagittal and coronal performance not extensively validated
2. Limited evaluation on pediatric patients; primarily trained on adult imaging
3. Performance may vary on severely artifact-prone images
4. Model does not differentiate between low-grade and high-grade gliomas (molecular subtyping not included)

Future Enhancements:
1. Integration of 3D volumetric analysis for tumor volume calculation
2. Molecular subtyping integration (IDH mutation status, 1p/19q codeletion prediction)
3. Multi-modal fusion (incorporating MR spectroscopy, perfusion imaging)
4. Longitudinal change detection for post-treatment surveillance
5. Radiomics feature extraction for prognostic prediction
6. Expansion to other tumor types (lymphoma, metastases, leptomeningeal involvement)
